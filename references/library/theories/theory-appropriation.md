# Appropriation, quotation, and parody

**In one plain sentence:** Artists often take a recognizable image, composition, or style
from elsewhere — quoting, sampling, or parodying it — so that the meaning comes partly from
what they kept and partly from what they changed.

**When it helps:** Reach for it whenever a work is clearly restaging something you recognize
(a famous composition, a genre convention, an earlier artist's move). Here, naming the
source is essential, not name-dropping: the whole point lives in the gap between the
borrowed frame and the new contents. Always read BOTH — what is faithfully kept, and what is
swapped — and ask what that swap does.

**How it gets misused:** Stopping at "this references X" as if spotting the source were the
insight. The insight is in the difference: why this composition, for this content, now?
Parody in particular can criticize, honor, or do both at once — don't assume mockery.

**Connects to:** any case where the artist borrows a known arrangement; pairs naturally with
the uncanny when the borrowed frame is made slightly wrong, and with the sublime or the
sacred when a solemn frame is given everyday contents.

**A question it opens:** You've kept the bones of something familiar but changed its body —
do you want the old frame to lend weight to the new contents, or to be punctured by them?
