# Association Engine

This explains how to let concepts, theories, other artworks, and real events come to
mind DURING a critique — and how to reach for them in the library — without turning the
conversation into a pile of name-dropping.

Read it together with:
- `vocabulary/feeling-words.md`, `vocabulary/tension-words.md`, and the form palette for the
  medium: `vocabulary/form-words.md` (visual/static) or `vocabulary/form-words-time-based.md`
  (audio/video/performance) — a video uses both
- `templates/` (what a card looks like)
- `library/index.md` (what's in the library and how the pieces connect)

---

## 1. Knowledge is a catalyst, not a verdict
- Theory, history, and other works are references you bring in to ask a better question
  and open things up — never to prove the artist is right or wrong because they resemble X.
- Every reference must be refusable. If the artist says the frame doesn't fit, let it go
  and try another.
- Keep it short: name the thing, say in one plain sentence what it is, and point to the one
  spot in THIS work that made you think of it. No mini-lectures.
- "More solid" means the conversation gets richer, not that you said more words.

## 2. The real question: is the link IN the work, or am I projecting it?
Before reaching for any reference, ask one thing:

> **Did the artist put this connection here (they borrowed, sampled, quoted, parodied, or
> followed a convention), or am I laying a generic resemblance onto a work that never
> invited it?**

- **If the connection is in the work**, naming the source is essential, not indulgent.
  A painting that restages Leonardo's *Last Supper* is ABOUT that restaging; missing it
  would be a failure, not restraint. Here, matching on motif and form is your MAIN job.
- **If you are projecting** (e.g. "it's a mountain, so let's talk about classical landscape"),
  that's where cliché lives. The guard against it is not banning a kind of association —
  it is the Relevance test in step 4: if you can't name a concrete spot in the work, drop it.

So all four ways of associating below are equal. What decides their value is whether they
are grounded in the work and in a concrete interface — not which "type" they are.

## 3. Four ways an association fires
| Way in | What sets it off | What you reach for |
|---|---|---|
| **Through a feeling** | a bodily/emotional response (you hold your breath, you flinch, it feels heavy) | other works/ideas that share that felt quality, even across unrelated subjects |
| **Through a form** | a making-move (an active empty space, a sudden shift in colour temperature, a severed edge) | works that make the same move, plus ideas about form |
| **Through a borrowing** | the work cites, samples, quotes, parodies, or follows a recognizable convention | the source it is drawing on, and what changes by re-staging it here |
| **Through a contradiction** | something that should not coexist does ("it's tender and violent at once") | a theory/case/event that can put a name to that gap |

All four are first-class. Feeling and contradiction tend to open the deepest dialogue;
borrowing is essential whenever the work is clearly quoting something; pure form-matching
is sharp but can feel cold, so pair it with the feeling it produces.

## 4. How to reach into the library (late, limited, in full sentences)
Don't pre-load references. Reach for one only when a point is actually live in the talk
(this matches the "one point per round" flow).

1. **Notice the signal** in the current point: a feeling, a making-move, a borrowing, or a
   contradiction. Use the plain words in `vocabulary/` to help you name it (the feeling and
   tension palettes are shared across media; for the making-move, use the form palette that
   matches the medium) — they are a palette for noticing, not codes you must paste.
2. **Check IN-work vs projected** (section 2). If projected and you can't ground it, stop.
3. **Scan summaries, then open 0–2.** Don't read every card. Scan the one-line `summary` of
   each card in one pass (grep `^summary:` across `library/`, or glob and read each card's
   top), then open at most **0–2** whose summary actually fits and read their full prose. See
   `library/index.md`. You are judging fit by sense, in plain language, not matching keywords.
4. **Apply the LRC test** (below); drop anything that fails Relevance.
5. **Reason it out in complete sentences, then ask a question.** See section 5.
6. **Write the artist's reaction** (they took it / pushed back / felt nothing) into the
   session notes, and let that decide whether to stay on this thread next round.

## 5. Reason in full sentences — never a string of fragments
When you bring an association into the conversation, say it as actual reasoning, in whole
sentences that show the chain of thought. Do NOT dump tags or clipped phrases.

The shape is roughly: **what it reminds you of → why it is the same thing (the concrete
spot in the work) → the question that opens up.**

- Avoid (fragment dump): "Uncanny. Last Supper. Tender-yet-violent. Negative space."
- Prefer (reasoned, in sentences):
  "The long table seen head-on, with the figures spread in a row and one centred, makes me
  think you're restaging Leonardo's *Last Supper*. What pulls at me is that the disciples
  have become animal-headed banqueters around a hotpot — the sacred composition is intact
  but its contents are swapped for something everyday and bodily. So I find myself asking:
  do you want the religious frame to dignify the feast, or do you want the feast to deflate
  the religious frame?"

That last sentence — an open question — is the point. The reasoning earns it.

## 6. A few rules to avoid one dominant story
- Stay poly-centric: the Western canon is one set of references among many. If the artist
  hasn't told you the context, ask or listen before you pick a frame.
- Tell settled common knowledge apart from contested readings. For contested ones, say
  "some people read it as…, does that spark anything for you?" rather than stating it as fact.
- Never let a theory overwrite what the artist is saying. Line up with their intent first,
  then set the outside language beside it.
- For event/fact cards, keep the source, date, region, and how sure you are.

## 7. Checking yourself during the session
- Few references, each tied to a concrete spot in the work, and at least half ending in a
  question.
- The artist ends up saying "this makes me think of X" or "I don't buy that comparison" —
  not just collecting names.
- If the artist is unmoved, go back to plainly describing what you see and feel. Don't pile
  on more reading.
